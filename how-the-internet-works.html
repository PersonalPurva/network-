🧭 The one-sentence mental model

The internet is not one machine — it is millions of separate networks agreeing to forward each other's messages. Your data is chopped into small packets, each stamped with a destination address, and every device in between only has one job: look at the address, pass it one hop closer. No single computer knows the whole path. That is the whole trick — and everything below is just the detail of how those hops happen.

1

The journey of one request

You open a website on your laptop, tethered to your phone's Airtel data. Here is every stop your request makes. Each stop is a concept we unpack later — this is the map of the whole page.

💻 Laptop (private IP)

Your device on the LAN. First it needs an IP for the name → DNS lookup.

📱 Phone as hotspot (NAT gateway)

Runs the tiny LAN + rewrites your private IP → public. This is the LAN/WAN border.

📡 Airtel cell tower (4G/5G radio)

Your entry into Airtel's WAN, over radio waves.

🏢 Airtel core network (CGNAT)

Routes traffic, assigns a shared public IP, applies a second layer of NAT.

🔀 National backbone / IXP

High-capacity fibre; Airtel hands your packet to peer networks.

🌊 Submarine cable landing

Your data physically leaves India through undersea fibre.

🌐 Destination ISP

A foreign network delivers the packet to the right building.

🖥️ Server (e.g. USA)

Receives request, sends the reply back down the same path in reverse.

Three things to notice

  • • Every arrow is two-way. The reply retraces the exact hops backward; NAT and CGNAT reverse their translations so it lands on your laptop.
  • • Nobody sees the whole route. Each hop only knows the next hop. This is why the internet survives cables being cut — packets reroute.
  • • It's fast. This entire round trip usually takes ~50–250 milliseconds, mostly limited by the speed of light in glass, not the computers.

⚡ Before any of this: DNS

You typed a name (google.com), but packets need a number (an IP address). So step zero is always a DNS lookup — a mini round-trip through this same path just to ask "what's the address for this name?" We cover it in section 4.

🛡️ Security preview

Every hop is a place an attacker could read, redirect, or block your data. That is why encryption (TLS) matters — it makes the middle hops carry data they can't read. Full attack map in section 9.

2

Networks by scope — LAN, WAN, and friends

A network is just devices that can talk to each other. We classify them by how far they reach. The key exam idea: LAN vs WAN is about scope and who controls it, not the size of the cables.

PAN

Personal Area Network

One person's devices, ~10 m. Bluetooth earbuds, smartwatch.

LAN ⭐

Local Area Network

One location you control — home, office, your phone's hotspot. Devices trust each other, use private IPs.

MAN

Metropolitan Area Network

City-sized — a campus, a cable-TV network across a town.

WAN ⭐

Wide Area Network

Spans cities, countries, oceans. The internet is the biggest WAN — a network of networks you don't control.

Your Airtel setup, labelled

🔵 The LAN part

Your laptop joins your phone's Wi-Fi hotspot. That hotspot is a LAN — just those two devices. They get private IPs like 192.168.43.5 and talk directly. The phone is the gateway: the one door out.

🟢 The WAN part

The moment your data crosses the phone into Airtel's radio network, it's on the WAN — Airtel's network, then other networks, then undersea to a foreign network. You control none of it; they all just agree to forward your packets.

A router that also does wireless (your phone hotspot, a home Wi-Fi box) is really 3 things in one: a switch (connects LAN devices), a router (finds the path out), and an access point (the radio). Good to separate these in your head — the exam does.

3

Addresses — IP, private vs public, and NAT

For packets to find each other, every device needs an address. There are actually two addresses in play at every moment — one for the local link, one for the whole internet.

MAC address (the hardware ID)

Burned into every network chip, e.g. A4:83:E7:2B:1C:90. Used only inside one LAN hop — "which physical device on this link?" It never travels across the internet. Changes at every hop.

IP address (the delivery address)

Logical, assigned by the network, e.g. 142.250.183.14. Used end-to-end across the whole internet — "which network, which host?" This is what routing uses.

Private IP

Works only inside a LAN. Reserved ranges you'll recognise: 10.x.x.x, 172.16–31.x.x, 192.168.x.x. Millions of homes reuse the same ones — that's fine, they never appear on the open internet.

Public IP

Globally unique, routable on the internet. Handed out (in blocks) by regional registries → ISPs. This is the address the outside world sees you as.

NAT — the translator at the border

NAT (Network Address Translation) is what your phone/router does at the LAN↔WAN border. It rewrites the packet's "from" address from your private IP to the one public IP, and keeps a table so replies come back to the right device.

OUT: 192.168.43.5:51000  →  rewritten to public-IP:51000  →  server

IN:  server  →  public-IP:51000  →  looked up in table  →  192.168.43.5

Why it exists: there are only ~4.3 billion IPv4 addresses — not enough for every device. NAT lets a whole household share one public IP. It also acts as an accidental firewall: unrequested inbound traffic has no table entry, so it's dropped.

CGNAT — why mobile is special

On Airtel mobile data, you usually sit behind CGNAT (Carrier-Grade NAT): thousands of subscribers share one public IP pool. So your "public IP" isn't really yours. That's two layers of NAT (phone + carrier) stacked.

IPv4 vs IPv6

IPv4 = 32-bit, ~4.3B addresses, running out. IPv6 = 128-bit (e.g. 2001:db8::1), effectively unlimited, so devices can have their own public address and skip NAT. Adoption is gradual; both run side by side today.

🛡️ Security note: because CGNAT shares one IP across many users, an IP address alone rarely identifies one person on mobile — relevant to logging, abuse tracking, and why "we traced the IP" is weaker evidence than TV suggests.

4

DNS — the internet's phonebook

You remember youtube.com; routers only understand numbers. DNS (Domain Name System) translates names → IP addresses. It happens before your request even starts.

The lookup, step by step

  1. Your device checks its own cache (did we look this up recently?).
  2. If not, it asks a resolver (usually your ISP's, e.g. Airtel's, or a public one like 8.8.8.8 / 1.1.1.1).
  3. The resolver asks a root server → "where's .com?"
  4. Then the .com TLD server → "where's youtube.com?"
  5. Then youtube's authoritative server → "here's the IP: 142.250.x.x."
  6. Resolver caches the answer (for its TTL) and hands it back. Now your real request begins.

Record types worth knowing

  • A — name → IPv4
  • AAAA — name → IPv6
  • CNAME — alias to another name
  • MX — mail server
  • NS — which DNS server is in charge
  • TXT — free text (used for SPF, verification)

🛡️ DNS attacks

  • • DNS spoofing / cache poisoning — feed a fake answer so a name points to an attacker's server.
  • • DNS hijacking — change which resolver you use.
  • • DNS tunnelling — smuggle stolen data out inside DNS queries.

🛡️ Defences

  • • DNSSEC — signs answers so forgeries are detected.
  • • DoH / DoT — DNS over HTTPS/TLS, so lookups are encrypted and can't be read or altered in transit.

The layers — how one message is built and unwrapped

The magic that makes independent networks cooperate is layering: each layer does one job and hands off to the next, wrapping the data in its own header like envelopes inside envelopes (encapsulation). The OSI model (7 layers) is the teaching model; the TCP/IP model (4 layers) is what the real internet runs on.

# OSI layer Job (plain words) Address / unit Real examples
7ApplicationWhat the user actually usesdataHTTP DNS SMTP
6PresentationFormat, encrypt, compressdataTLS JPEG UTF-8
5SessionOpen/keep/close conversationsdatasockets
4TransportReliability, ports, orderingsegment · portTCP UDP
3NetworkFind the path across networkspacket · IPIP ICMP routers
2Data LinkMove over one physical hopframe · MACEthernet Wi-Fi switches
1PhysicalThe actual signalsbitsfibre, radio, copper

Encapsulation — envelopes in envelopes

Going down the stack on send, each layer adds its header:

[ Ethernet/Wi-Fi [ IP [ TCP [ HTTP data ] ] ] ]
L2       L3  L4  L7

The receiver unwraps in reverse (de-encapsulation). At every router hop, the L2 wrapper is stripped and rewritten (new MAC), but the L3 IP stays the same end-to-end. That single fact explains most of networking.

TCP/IP model — the real one

The internet actually uses 4 layers. Map them onto OSI:

  • Application = OSI 5–7
  • Transport = OSI 4
  • Internet = OSI 3
  • Link = OSI 1–2

Mnemonic for OSI 1→7: “Please Do Not Throw Sausage Pizza Away.”

6

Packets, routing, ports & the two transports

Why packets, not one big stream?

Your data is split into small packets. Each carries source IP, destination IP, a sequence number, and a chunk of data. Benefits: they can take different routes, a lost one is re-sent alone, and many users share the same lines (packet switching). Old phone systems used circuit switching — a dedicated line per call; the internet doesn't.

How routers choose the path

Each router holds a routing table and forwards each packet to the next hop that's closest to the destination network. Between big networks, BGP (Border Gateway Protocol) advertises "you can reach these IP ranges through me." TTL (time-to-live) counts down each hop so lost packets die instead of looping forever. traceroute reveals these hops.

TCP — reliable

  • • Handshake first (SYN → SYN-ACK → ACK) before any data.
  • • Guarantees order and delivery; re-sends lost packets.
  • • Use when correctness matters: web pages, email, file downloads.

UDP — fast

  • • No handshake, no re-sends — just fire packets.
  • • Lower latency, but some loss is possible.
  • • Use when speed > perfection: video calls, live streaming, gaming, DNS.

Ports — many conversations on one IP

An IP gets your packet to the right machine; a port gets it to the right program on that machine. Your browser, mail app, and game each use different ports so replies don't get mixed up.

20/21 FTP22 SSH25 SMTP53 DNS80 HTTP143 IMAP443 HTTPS3389 RDP

🛡️ Port scanning (e.g. nmap) is step one of most pentests — you map which ports are open to see what services a target is running.

The physical internet — who owns the wires

"The cloud" is other people's computers connected by very real cables. Here's the physical hierarchy your Airtel packet climbs.

🏠

Access network

How you reach your ISP: mobile radio (your case), fibre-to-the-home, cable, DSL. The "last mile."

🏢

Tier 3 / Tier 2 ISPs

Retail and regional providers (Airtel's retail side, JioFiber, ACT). They buy transit from bigger networks to reach the whole internet.

🔀

IXP — Internet Exchange Point

A shared building where many networks peer (swap traffic directly, often free) instead of paying to route the long way. Keeps local traffic local and fast.

🌍

Tier 1 networks

The backbone giants that can reach the entire internet without paying anyone — they only peer with each other. They own the intercontinental links.

🌊

Submarine cables

~99% of intercontinental traffic runs through fibre-optic cables on the seabed — not satellites (too slow/limited for bulk traffic). India connects via landing stations at Mumbai, Chennai and others. This is the literal "international connection."

📦

CDNs & data centres

Big sites cache copies close to you (Cloudflare, Akamai, Google edge). Often your "US server" request is quietly served from a box inside India — faster, and it never crosses the ocean at all.

🛡️ Why this matters for security & resilience: cutting a few submarine cables can slow a whole region; BGP mistakes/attacks can reroute a country's traffic through the wrong network. The physical + routing layer is real-world national infrastructure.

8

HTTP, HTTPS & TLS — the web on top

Everything above delivers bytes anywhere. The web is just one application riding on top, spoken in HTTP.

HTTP — request & response

Your browser sends a request (GET /page) and the server sends a response (status + content). Common methods: GET (read), POST (send data), PUT, DELETE. Status codes: 200 OK, 301 moved, 404 not found, 403 forbidden, 500 server error.

HTTPS = HTTP + TLS 🔒

Plain HTTP is readable by every hop in between. TLS wraps it so those hops carry data they can't read or alter. The padlock means the traffic is encrypted and the server proved its identity with a certificate.

What the TLS handshake gives you (3 guarantees)

🔐 Confidentiality

Encrypted, so eavesdroppers on any hop see only scrambled bytes.

✅ Integrity

Tampering is detected — data can't be silently changed in transit.

🆔 Authentication

A certificate (signed by a trusted CA) proves you're talking to the real server, not an impostor.

Under the hood it uses asymmetric crypto (public/private keys) to agree on a shared secret, then switches to fast symmetric encryption for the actual data. This is exactly the applied-crypto core of your security syllabus.

Your red-team lens: every layer you just learned is an attack surface. This table is the one to internalise — it maps the whole page onto offensive/defensive security.

The CIA triad ties it together — every defence protects one of: Confidentiality (keep secret), Integrity (keep unaltered), Availability (keep reachable). When you analyse any attack, ask which of the three it breaks. That single habit is the backbone of security thinking.

Quick cheat sheets

Acronyms in one place

  • LAN/WAN/MAN/PAN — network by scope
  • IP — Internet Protocol address
  • NAT/CGNAT — address translation
  • DNS — name → IP
  • DHCP — auto-assigns IPs on a LAN
  • TCP/UDP — reliable / fast transport
  • ISP — Internet Service Provider
  • IXP — Internet Exchange Point
  • BGP — routing between networks
  • TLS/SSL — encryption for HTTPS
  • CDN — cached copies near you

The full round trip (recite this)

  1. Type name → DNS resolves to IP
  2. Data split into packets
  3. TCP handshake + TLS handshake
  4. Laptop → phone (LAN, private IP)
  5. NAT → tower → CGNAT (WAN)
  6. Backbone → IXP → submarine cable
  7. Foreign ISP → server
  8. Reply retraces path; NAT reverses; browser renders

Handy commands to explore

  • ipconfig — see your IP (Win)
  • ping — is a host reachable?
  • tracert — show every hop
  • nslookup — do a DNS query
  • netstat — open connections/ports

Try tracert google.com on your laptop — you'll literally see the hops from this page.
